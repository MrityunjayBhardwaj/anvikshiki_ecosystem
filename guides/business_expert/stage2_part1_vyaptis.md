# STAGE 2: KNOWLEDGE ARCHITECTURE

## Step 2A: Vyāpti List (Structural Regularities)

```
VYĀPTI 1: The Value Equation
Statement: A business survives if and only if it creates more 
  economic value than it consumes in resources, as measured by 
  the unit economics of its core transaction — and the rate at 
  which this surplus can be reinvested determines the trajectory.
Causal status:
  ☑ CAUSAL — Intervening on unit economics (improving LTV/CAC 
    ratio, reducing COGS, increasing pricing power) reliably 
    changes business trajectory. A business with negative unit 
    economics that does not fix them will die when capital runs out.
Domain type form: Type 4 pattern-to-outcome: "In situations 
  where unit economics are structurally negative, no amount of 
  growth fixes the problem — growth accelerates death."
Scope conditions: Holds universally for commercial enterprises. 
  Breaks down for: subsidized entities (government, nonprofit), 
  marketplace businesses during network-effect building phase 
  (temporarily rational to subsidize), and platform businesses 
  where the "unit" is ambiguous.
Why it matters: Once grasped, the reader can evaluate ANY 
  business by asking: "What is the core transaction, what does 
  it cost to produce, and what does the customer pay?" — and 
  derive strategic implications from the answer.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 2 (Unit Economics & the Core Transaction)
```

```
VYĀPTI 2: The Constraint Cascade
Statement: In any business system, performance is determined by 
  the binding constraint — the single bottleneck that limits 
  throughput. Optimizing non-binding constraints produces zero 
  marginal improvement. The binding constraint shifts as you 
  relieve it, creating a cascade.
Causal status:
  ☑ CAUSAL — Intervening on the binding constraint improves 
    system performance; intervening on non-binding constraints 
    does not. (Theory of Constraints, Goldratt)
Domain type form: Type 4: "If your startup is bottlenecked on 
  engineering talent, spending more on marketing produces zero 
  additional customers served."
Scope conditions: Holds for systems with serial dependencies. 
  Weakens in highly parallel systems where multiple independent 
  bottlenecks exist simultaneously. Also weakens when the 
  "constraint" is poorly identified (common failure mode).
Why it matters: Prevents the most common resource allocation 
  error — spreading investment across all areas instead of 
  concentrating on the constraint.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 3 (Constraint Structure & Resource Allocation)
```

```
VYĀPTI 3: The Information Asymmetry Premium
Statement: In any transaction or competitive interaction, the 
  party with superior information about the true state of 
  affairs captures disproportionate value — and the structure 
  of information asymmetry determines market structure, pricing 
  power, and strategic advantage.
Causal status:
  ☑ STRUCTURAL — Information asymmetry and value capture are 
    connected by market architecture (adverse selection, moral 
    hazard, signaling mechanisms), not direct causation. The 
    structural link is that uninformed parties must either 
    accept worse terms, invest in information acquisition, or 
    exit.
Domain type form: Type 4 + Type 3: Pattern observed across 
  all markets; the mechanism is well-understood (Akerlof, Spence, 
  Stiglitz) but the specific manifestation varies by context.
Scope conditions: Holds in all markets with heterogeneous 
  quality. Weakens in perfectly commoditized markets where 
  quality is verifiable. Also weakens when regulatory 
  intervention forces information disclosure.
Why it matters: Explains why moats exist, why some businesses 
  have pricing power and others don't, and why the best 
  strategic positions involve creating or exploiting 
  information advantages.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 5 (Competitive Position & Moat)
```

```
VYĀPTI 4: The Organizational Entropy Principle
Statement: Organizations naturally accumulate coordination 
  overhead, misaligned incentives, and information loss as they 
  grow. Without active intervention (clear structure, aligned 
  incentives, information systems), organizational effectiveness 
  degrades — and this degradation accelerates at predictable 
  scale thresholds (roughly 8, 50, 150, 500 people).
Causal status:
  ☑ EMPIRICAL REGULARITY — The scale thresholds are robustly 
    observed (Dunbar's number, span of control research, startup 
    scaling literature) but the precise mechanism at each 
    threshold is a combination of communication overhead (n² 
    channels), cognitive limits, and cultural dilution. The 
    causal pathway is partially but not fully understood.
Domain type form: Type 4 + Type 3: Observed pattern with 
  known moderators (organizational design quality, communication 
  tools, cultural strength) but no precise causal model.
Scope conditions: Holds for all human organizations. The 
  specific thresholds shift with organizational design choices 
  (e.g., Amazon's two-pizza teams push one threshold higher). 
  May be disrupted by AI agents replacing human coordination.
Why it matters: Tells a founder WHEN their current management 
  approach will break, and what structural changes are needed 
  before it does.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 7 (Organizational Design & Scaling)
```

```
VYĀPTI 5: The Market Signal Decay Law
Statement: The further a decision-maker is from the customer, 
  the more distorted the market signal becomes — through 
  aggregation, filtering, interpretation bias, and reporting 
  incentives. Strategic errors correlate with signal decay.
Causal status:
  ☑ CAUSAL — Intervening on signal fidelity (moving executives 
    closer to customers, removing reporting layers, using direct 
    data) reliably improves decision quality. The mechanism is 
    information loss through successive compression and 
    motivated filtering.
Domain type form: Type 4: "If the CEO's understanding of 
  customer needs is entirely mediated through the VP of Sales' 
  summary of the Sales team's summary of customer conversations, 
  the signal has been compressed and filtered three times."
Scope conditions: Holds universally. Strength varies — stronger 
  in large organizations with deep hierarchies; weaker in small 
  teams where the founder talks to customers directly. Partially 
  mitigated by data infrastructure but never eliminated (data 
  aggregation is itself a form of signal compression).
Why it matters: Explains why large companies miss disruption, 
  why founder-led companies often have better product-market 
  fit, and why "talk to your customers" is the most 
  underweight advice in business.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 9 (Market & Customer Reasoning)
```

```
VYĀPTI 6: The Optionality-Commitment Tradeoff
Statement: Every strategic commitment (hiring, investment, 
  partnership, market entry) simultaneously creates capability 
  and destroys optionality. The quality of strategic judgment 
  is determined by the accuracy of the tradeoff between the 
  value of what you gain (committed capability) and the value 
  of what you lose (foreclosed alternatives).
Causal status:
  ☑ STRUCTURAL — Commitment and optionality are connected 
    by the structure of decision trees: choosing one branch 
    prunes others. The value of optionality is determined by 
    uncertainty about which branch is best.
Domain type form: Type 4: "The decision to raise venture 
  capital creates capability (cash, credibility, network) and 
  destroys optionality (you now have investors with 
  expectations, a liquidation preference, and a ticking clock)."
Scope conditions: Universal in resource-constrained decision-
  making. Weakens when resources are abundant relative to 
  opportunity cost (rare). The tradeoff is always present but 
  its sharpness varies — irreversible decisions (M&A, key 
  hires, market entry) have sharper tradeoffs than reversible 
  ones (marketing spend, pricing experiments).
Why it matters: This is the CORE structure of executive 
  judgment — every significant decision is an optionality-
  commitment tradeoff, and expertise is largely about 
  evaluating these tradeoffs accurately under uncertainty.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 10 (Executive Judgment & Synthesis)
```

```
VYĀPTI 7: The Incentive-Behavior Isomorphism
Statement: In any organization, actual behavior converges to 
  what is actually incentivized (measured, rewarded, punished) 
  — regardless of stated values, mission statements, or 
  managerial intentions. Culture is the emergent property of 
  incentive structures and hiring filters, not a separately 
  controllable variable.
Causal status:
  ☑ CAUSAL — Intervening on incentive structures (what gets 
    measured, promoted, rewarded) reliably changes organizational 
    behavior. Intervening on stated values without changing 
    incentives does not. (Agency theory, principal-agent 
    literature, Kerr's "On the Folly of Rewarding A While 
    Hoping for B")
Domain type form: Type 4 + Type 3: Strong causal evidence from 
  organizational behavior research; the mechanism (rational 
  agents responding to incentive gradients) is well-understood.
Scope conditions: Holds universally with slight moderation by 
  intrinsic motivation (people sometimes act against incentives 
  for values-based reasons, but this is unreliable at 
  organizational scale). Breaks down as a DESIGN tool when 
  the desired behavior is too complex to measure — Goodhart's 
  Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a 
  good measure."
Why it matters: Explains why organizations behave contrary to 
  their stated values, why culture change initiatives fail 
  without incentive restructuring, and provides the primary 
  lever for organizational design.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 8 (Incentive Design & Culture)
```

```
VYĀPTI 8: The Capital Allocation Identity
Statement: Over long periods, a company's value is determined 
  almost entirely by how its CEO allocates capital — the 
  cumulative decisions about where cash goes (reinvestment, 
  acquisitions, dividends, buybacks, debt paydown) determine 
  returns more than any single strategic bet. Capital 
  allocation skill is the highest-leverage executive function.
Causal status:
  ☑ CAUSAL — Intervening on capital allocation decisions 
    (redirecting investment from low-return to high-return 
    activities) reliably changes long-term business value. 
    (Thorndike's "The Outsiders"; Buffett's annual letters)
Domain type form: Type 4: "The CEO who consistently allocates 
  capital to the highest-return opportunities compounds value; 
  the CEO who allocates politically or inertially destroys it."
Scope conditions: Holds strongly for mature businesses with 
  significant free cash flow. For startups, the principle 
  holds but the mechanism is different — capital allocation 
  means choosing between product development, hiring, marketing, 
  and runway preservation, not between dividends and buybacks. 
  Weakens when the CEO has limited allocation discretion 
  (regulated industries, investor-imposed constraints).
Why it matters: Reframes the CEO's job from "making strategy" 
  to "allocating resources" — a more precise and actionable 
  framing that connects financial reasoning to strategic 
  reasoning.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 4 (Capital Allocation & Financial Strategy)
```

```
VYĀPTI 9: The Disruption Asymmetry
Statement: Incumbent firms systematically underinvest in 
  technologies/approaches that initially serve only low-margin 
  or non-consumption markets, because rational resource 
  allocation within the incumbent's business model directs 
  investment toward existing high-margin customers. This 
  creates a structural opening for entrants who can build 
  capability in the low-end market and then move upmarket.
Causal status:
  ☑ CAUSAL — The mechanism is rational resource allocation 
    within existing incentive structures: managers who allocate 
    resources to existing high-margin customers are rewarded; 
    managers who invest in unproven low-margin markets are not. 
    Intervening on the decision framework (creating independent 
    units with different metrics) can counter this.
Domain type form: Type 4 + Type 3: Christensen's disruption 
  theory, supported by extensive case evidence but contested 
  in its universality. The causal mechanism is clear; the 
  scope conditions are debated.
Scope conditions: Strongest in industries with sustaining 
  innovation trajectories where incumbent products overshoot 
  customer needs. Weakens when: the incumbent is attentive to 
  disruption (rare but possible), when the disruption attack 
  vector requires scale the entrant cannot build, or when 
  regulatory barriers protect the incumbent.
Why it matters: As an AI startup, the reader IS the potential 
  disruptor. Understanding this asymmetry from both sides — 
  how to exploit it as an entrant, and how incumbents fail 
  to respond — is directly strategic for the reader.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 6 (Business Model Design & Innovation)
```

```
VYĀPTI 10: The Judgment Calibration Principle
Statement: The quality of executive judgment is determined 
  less by the quantity of knowledge and more by the accuracy 
  of the decision-maker's self-assessment — knowing what you 
  know vs. what you don't, and being calibrated about the 
  confidence warranted by your evidence. Overconfident 
  executives make catastrophic bets; underconfident executives 
  miss critical windows.
Causal status:
  ☑ EMPIRICAL REGULARITY — Research on expert calibration 
    (Tetlock, Kahneman) shows that calibration quality predicts 
    decision quality, but the causal pathway runs through 
    multiple mechanisms (information seeking, risk assessment, 
    willingness to update). The regularity is robust; the 
    precise causal model is complex.
Domain type form: Type 4 + Type 3: The pattern is observed 
  across domains; the mechanism involves Bayesian updating 
  quality, confirmation bias resistance, and epistemic humility.
Scope conditions: Holds universally for decisions under 
  uncertainty. Weakens for decisions under (near-)certainty 
  where calibration matters less. Interacts with domain — 
  calibration is easier in domains with fast, clear feedback 
  (trading) and harder in domains with slow, ambiguous feedback 
  (strategy).
Why it matters: This is the META-vyāpti — it governs the 
  reader's ability to use all other vyāptis correctly. It is 
  also the reader's strongest existing skill (Bayesian 
  reasoning + uncertainty quantification) applied to a new 
  domain.
Chapter it anchors: Chapter 11 (The Expert's Framework)
```
