Structural patterns I suspect might transfer:

- Posterior predictive checks → Business forecasting and validation (checking whether your mental model actually predicts what happens)
- Uncertainty propagation → Risk management (how uncertainty in one business assumption cascades through financial projections, strategy, and operations)
- Basis functions and span → Core business competencies: a small set of "basis" skills (financial literacy, strategic reasoning, people judgment, operational sense) that span the full space of executive decision-making. An MBA is a particular choice of basis; a founder's intuition is a different basis for the same space. The question is: what's the minimal spanning set?
- Change of basis → Reframing: the same business situation looks fundamentally different when projected onto a financial basis (cash flow, margins) vs. a strategic basis (competitive position, moat) vs. an operational basis (execution capacity, bottlenecks). Expert executives perform change-of-basis fluidly — they see the same problem from multiple representations and know which representation makes the decision structure most visible
- Representation space → How business knowledge is encoded: an experienced CEO's "representation" of a market is a compressed, high-dimensional space where similar situations cluster
- Kernel / null space → What a business framework can't see: every strategic framework (Porter's, BCG matrix, etc.) has a null space — classes of situations it projects to zero. Knowing a framework's kernel is knowing its blind spots
- Eigendecomposition → Principal modes of business variation: in any industry, a few "eigenvectors" (key dimensions like margin structure, customer concentration, regulatory exposure) explain most of the variance in outcomes. Expert pattern-matching is essentially projecting onto the dominant eigenvectors
- Projection and orthogonality → Decomposing business problems into independent components: the CFO sees the financial projection, the COO sees the operational projection — these are ideally orthogonal views of the same underlying business state
- Topological invariants → Organizational structure (what properties of an org are preserved under transformation/growth — what's structurally robust vs. fragile)
- Persistent homology → Market structure analysis (identifying persistent features of competitive landscapes vs. transient noise)
- Loss functions and optimization → What executives actually optimize for (and the multi-objective nature of real business — you can't reduce it to a single loss)
