# STAGE 2 — Part 2: Hetvābhāsas (Characteristic Fallacies)

## Step 2B: Hetvābhāsa List

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 1: The Revenue Vanity Trap
The fallacy: Inferring business health from revenue growth alone, 
  ignoring unit economics, cash flow, and margin structure. 
  "Revenue is growing 200% YoY, therefore the business is succeeding."
Why it's attractive: Revenue growth IS a signal of market demand 
  and execution capability. It feels like the most important 
  number. Media, investors, and peers all celebrate revenue milestones.
Why it's wrong: Revenue without positive unit economics is a 
  measure of how fast you're spending capital, not how much value 
  you're creating. WeWork grew revenue explosively while destroying 
  billions in value. Revenue growth with negative gross margins means 
  every additional sale makes the company worse off.
Counter-example: WeWork (2019) — $1.8B revenue, -$1.6B net loss. 
  MoviePass — millions of subscribers, negative unit economics on 
  every ticket. Both showed extraordinary "growth" while accelerating 
  toward collapse.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 2 (Unit Economics) — 
  this is where the reader first encounters value creation vs. 
  revenue, and is at maximum risk of conflating them.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 2: The Framework Reification Error
The fallacy: Treating strategic frameworks (Porter's Five Forces, 
  BCG Matrix, SWOT, TAM-SAM-SOM) as causal models of reality 
  rather than lossy heuristic compressions. "The Five Forces 
  analysis shows our industry is attractive, therefore we should enter."
Why it's attractive: Frameworks provide structure to ambiguous 
  situations. They feel rigorous. Business schools teach them as 
  if they were analytical tools with deterministic outputs.
Why it's wrong: Every framework is a projection that has a kernel 
  (null space) — classes of situations it maps to zero. Porter's 
  Five Forces doesn't capture ecosystem dynamics, platform effects, 
  or regulatory arbitrage. The BCG Matrix assumes market share 
  determines profitability (it often doesn't). Using any single 
  framework as if it captured the full structure is like using a 
  single basis vector to represent a high-dimensional space.
Counter-example: Nokia's Five Forces analysis of the mobile market 
  in 2006 would have looked excellent — strong supplier power, high 
  barriers to entry, limited substitution threat. The analysis 
  was correct within its projection but blind to the category 
  redefinition that the iPhone represented.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 5 (Competitive 
  Position & Moat) — where frameworks are introduced and the reader 
  is most tempted to over-rely on them.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 3: The Survivorship Inference
The fallacy: Studying successful companies to extract principles 
  of success, without examining companies that followed the same 
  principles and failed. "Amazon succeeded by prioritizing 
  long-term thinking over short-term profits, therefore long-term 
  thinking drives success."
Why it's attractive: Success stories are vivid, available, and 
  emotionally compelling. We can study Amazon, Apple, and Google 
  in detail. We cannot easily study the thousands of companies 
  that prioritized long-term thinking and went bankrupt.
Why it's wrong: Without the base rate (how many companies tried 
  this approach and failed?), the sample is systematically biased. 
  The "principles" extracted may be necessary conditions, 
  sufficient conditions, or neither — survivorship bias 
  prevents distinguishing these.
Counter-example: The "Built to Last" companies (Collins & Porras, 
  1994) — of the 18 companies identified as "visionary," several 
  subsequently underperformed or collapsed (Circuit City, Motorola). 
  The principles extracted from their success didn't predict their 
  subsequent failure because they were artifacts of survivorship 
  bias in the original sample.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 6 (Business Model 
  Design) — where the reader begins evaluating business models 
  and is tempted to pattern-match from famous successes.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 4: The Correlation-Strategy Confusion
The fallacy: Observing that successful companies share a feature 
  and inferring that the feature causes success, without 
  establishing the causal mechanism or ruling out confounders. 
  "Successful startups have strong cultures, therefore building 
  a strong culture causes startup success."
Why it's attractive: Correlational patterns are real and visible. 
  The mind naturally constructs causal narratives from 
  correlational data — this is a deep cognitive bias, not a 
  sign of carelessness.
Why it's wrong: The causal direction may be reversed (success 
  enables culture investment, not vice versa), or both may 
  share a common cause (strong founding teams produce both 
  culture and success independently). Without intervening on 
  the proposed cause and observing the downstream effect, the 
  causal claim is unsupported.
Counter-example: "Companies with open office plans are more 
  innovative" — the correlation is real, but the causal 
  mechanism is likely reversed: innovative companies (tech 
  startups, design firms) adopt open plans as a cultural 
  signal. Imposing open plans on a non-innovative company 
  doesn't produce innovation; it reduces productivity.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 1 (Financial 
  Reasoning Foundations) — early, to establish causal 
  reasoning discipline before the reader encounters strategic 
  claims.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 5: The Scalability Presumption
The fallacy: Assuming that what worked at scale N will work at 
  scale 10N, or that a market validated at small scale will 
  behave the same at large scale. "We have 100 paying customers 
  who love the product, therefore the TAM of 10,000 similar 
  customers is addressable with the same approach."
Why it's attractive: Linear extrapolation is the default mental 
  model. If something works for 100 customers and there are 
  10,000 potential customers, the math seems straightforward. 
  Investors actively encourage this reasoning (TAM-SAM-SOM).
Why it's wrong: Phase transitions exist. What works for 100 
  customers may require fundamentally different infrastructure, 
  sales processes, support models, and organizational capabilities 
  at 1,000. The mechanism changes — direct founder-sales 
  becomes sales-team-sales, which has different economics. 
  Customer heterogeneity increases with scale. The early 
  adopters who loved the MVP may be structurally different 
  from the mainstream market.
Counter-example: Many SaaS startups validated with founder-led 
  sales to 50–100 design partners, then failed when attempting 
  to scale through a sales team — because the founder's 
  technical credibility and relationship depth were the product, 
  not the software.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 7 (Organizational 
  Design & Scaling) — where scaling dynamics are central.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 6: The Sunk Cost Anchor
The fallacy: Continuing to invest in a failing strategy because 
  of prior investment, rather than evaluating the marginal 
  return of the next dollar invested. "We've already spent $2M 
  on this product line — we can't abandon it now."
Why it's attractive: Loss aversion is a deep psychological bias. 
  Abandoning a prior investment feels like admitting failure. 
  Organizational politics reinforces this — the people who 
  championed the investment have careers tied to its continuation.
Why it's wrong: The $2M is gone regardless of what you do next. 
  The only relevant question is: does the NEXT dollar invested 
  here produce more value than the NEXT dollar invested 
  elsewhere? Prior expenditure is irrelevant to this marginal 
  analysis.
Counter-example: The Concorde (sunk cost fallacy is literally 
  called "the Concorde fallacy"). British and French governments 
  continued funding despite clear evidence the project would never 
  be commercially viable, because of prior investment and 
  national prestige. In business: Kodak's continued investment 
  in film technology despite clear market shift to digital.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 4 (Capital 
  Allocation) — where resource allocation decisions are central 
  and the sunk cost trap is the most common allocation error.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 7: The Moat Mirage
The fallacy: Claiming a competitive advantage exists because 
  you currently have no competitors, or because your technology 
  is temporarily superior. "We have no competitors in this 
  space" does not mean you have a moat — it may mean the 
  market doesn't exist or isn't worth entering.
Why it's attractive: The absence of competition feels like 
  dominance. A technology lead feels durable. First-mover 
  advantage is a widely cited (but poorly understood) concept.
Why it's wrong: A moat requires STRUCTURAL barriers to 
  competition — network effects, switching costs, economies 
  of scale, regulatory capture, or accumulated data/knowledge 
  advantages. A technology lead without these structural 
  barriers is a head start, not a moat. Head starts evaporate; 
  moats persist.
Counter-example: Friendster had a first-mover advantage in 
  social networking and no competitors. It had no moat — no 
  network effects strong enough to prevent switching, no 
  proprietary data advantage, no switching costs. MySpace 
  then Facebook displaced it trivially.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 5 (Competitive 
  Position & Moat) — where moat evaluation is the core skill 
  being built, and this is the most common novice error.
```

```
HETVĀBHĀSA 8: The Metric Goodhart
The fallacy: Optimizing for a measurable proxy and treating 
  improvement in the proxy as improvement in the underlying 
  goal. Named for Goodhart's Law: "When a measure becomes a 
  target, it ceases to be a good measure."
Why it's attractive: Metrics provide clarity, accountability, 
  and a sense of progress. Organizations need measurable 
  targets. Without metrics, management feels subjective and 
  unfair.
Why it's wrong: The metric and the goal are never identical. 
  Optimizing the metric creates incentives to improve the 
  metric through means that don't improve — or actively harm — 
  the underlying goal. This is a structural consequence of the 
  Information Asymmetry vyāpti: the agent has more information 
  about HOW the metric is being achieved than the principal.
Counter-example: Wells Fargo optimized for "accounts opened 
  per customer" as a proxy for customer relationship depth. 
  Employees created millions of unauthorized accounts — the 
  metric improved while the actual goal (customer 
  relationships) was destroyed.
Where in the guide to introduce it: Chapter 8 (Incentive 
  Design & Culture) — where the interaction between incentives, 
  metrics, and behavior is the central topic.
```
