## PHASE 0: DOMAIN AUDIT
### (The Pramāṇa Question — How Do We Know Things Here?)

### → DOMAIN CLASSIFICATION OUTPUT:

- **Primary domain type:** TYPE 4 — PRACTICAL/CRAFT
  - Business strategy and executive decision-making is fundamentally a domain of skilled practice. Truth here is pragmatic — what reliably works in what contexts, verified by experienced practitioners and (increasingly) by systematic empirical study. Expert judgment is a valid and irreplaceable cognitive tool. The domain's knowledge is not primarily theorem-like (Type 1) or experiment-like (Type 2), but pattern-to-outcome relationships refined through practice under conditions of uncertainty, incomplete information, and time pressure.

- **Secondary domain types:**
  - **Type 3 (Empirical/Probabilistic):** Financial reasoning, market dynamics, and organizational behavior have a substantial empirical evidence base. Effect sizes, replication, and statistical reasoning matter for claims about "what works" in strategy and management. The guide must handle this sub-layer — especially around causal claims — with probabilistic rigor.
  - **Type 5 (Interpretive):** Strategic analysis and organizational culture have interpretive dimensions. Multiple valid readings of the same competitive landscape can coexist. The "narrative construction" aspect of strategy (how CEOs frame situations to create alignment) is partly interpretive.

- **Implication for "first principles":** First principles here are NOT axioms (Type 1) or conservation laws (Type 2). They are:
  1. **The goal structure** — What is the business trying to achieve, and for whom? (Value creation, capture, and distribution)
  2. **The constraint structure** — What bounds the business? (Capital, time, information, talent, market structure, regulatory environment)
  3. **The failure mode catalogue** — What causes businesses to fail, and how? (Misallocation, misreading markets, organizational dysfunction, judgment errors)
  
  These three — goals, constraints, failure modes — are the bedrock from which all strategic reasoning derives.

- **Implication for "expert endpoint":** The reader will be able to:
  - Exercise judgment about novel business situations under time pressure and incomplete information
  - Read financial statements and extract strategic signal (not just mechanics)
  - Evaluate competitive positions, business models, and strategic decisions using a set of structural regularities
  - Diagnose organizational dysfunction and business problems using a systematic failure-mode framework
  - Know which patterns apply in a given situation, where they break down, and when to override frameworks with first-principles reasoning
  - Distinguish genuine expertise from confident bluffing in business contexts

- **Implication for valid knowledge claims (pramāṇa):** What counts as valid knowledge in business strategy:
  1. **Practitioner experience systematically documented** — Case studies, post-mortems, earnings calls where executives explain reasoning. The weight of evidence from hundreds of practitioners facing similar situations is strong pramāṇa.
  2. **Empirical research with appropriate scope conditions** — Academic studies on strategy, organizational behavior, and financial performance. Strong when replicated, with known moderators. Weak when sample-dependent or context-specific without acknowledged scope limits.
  3. **Financial and market data** — Observable, quantitative evidence of business performance. Strong pramāṇa for "what happened"; requires interpretation for "why it happened."
  4. **Expert judgment under defined conditions** — The informed opinion of experienced practitioners who have seen hundreds of cases. Valid pramāṇa when the practitioner can articulate scope conditions and failure modes of their judgment. Weak pramāṇa when the practitioner confuses pattern-matching with fundamental law.
  5. **Structural reasoning from constraints** — Logical deduction from the goal structure, constraint structure, and known mechanisms (e.g., "if margins are negative and capital is finite, the business must either find margin improvement or raise capital or die" — this follows from basic accounting identities). Strong pramāṇa; closest to Type 1 reasoning in this domain.

  What does NOT count as strong pramāṇa: single anecdotes ("Bezos did X, therefore X works"), survivorship-biased success stories, frameworks treated as causal laws rather than heuristic compressions, and self-reported "best practices" without outcome data.

### → FAILURE ONTOLOGY OUTPUT:

- **What counts as failure:**
  - Failure is **graded, not binary**. A business can be partially failing (e.g., growing revenue but destroying value through negative unit economics), failing on one dimension while succeeding on another (e.g., gaining market share while losing talent), or failing slowly in ways that only become visible after it's too late to correct.
  - Failure is **perspectival** — what looks like failure to investors (declining returns) may look like necessary investment to the founder (building infrastructure for future scale). What looks like strategic failure to analysts (losing market share) may be deliberate repositioning.
  - Some failures are **reversible** (a bad hire, a misguided marketing campaign) and some are **irreversible** (running out of cash, catastrophic reputation damage, losing a key patent dispute). The guide must distinguish between these because the decision calculus is fundamentally different.

- **Failure relative to what:**
  - **Primary benchmark:** Value creation vs. value destruction — is the enterprise generating more value (for customers, shareholders, employees) than it consumes in resources? This is the fundamental economic test.
  - **Whose benchmark matters depends on the decision:** Investors measure failure against return expectations and opportunity cost. Customers measure failure against their needs being met. Employees measure failure against career development and working conditions. The CEO must integrate all of these — which is why "executive judgment" is the meta-skill.
  - **The competitive benchmark:** A business can be doing well in absolute terms but failing relative to competitors who are growing faster, innovating more effectively, or capturing more market value. Failure is always partly relative to alternatives.

- **Failure on what timescale:**
  - **Short-term success → Long-term failure:** Classic pattern: over-optimizing for current quarter earnings while underinvesting in R&D, talent, or infrastructure. Success at T1 (hitting revenue targets) becomes failure at T2 (product stagnation, key talent departure, competitive vulnerability).
  - **Short-term failure → Long-term success:** Amazon operated at a loss for years while building infrastructure. Netflix destroyed its DVD business to build streaming. What looks like failure at T1 (negative margins, subscriber loss) can be strategic investment for T2.
  - **The timescale trap:** One of the most common executive errors is applying the wrong time horizon. Startups that optimize for long-term vision without validating short-term unit economics fail. Enterprises that optimize for quarterly metrics without investing in capabilities fail on a longer horizon. Both are failures of timescale reasoning.

- **Failure vs. insufficient information:**
  - **The information horizon problem:** In business, you almost never have enough information to know definitively whether a strategy has failed or simply hasn't had enough time to work. A product launch may fail because the product is wrong (actual failure) or because the go-to-market took time to build pipeline (insufficient information). Distinguishing these is a core expert skill.
  - **Leading indicators vs. lagging indicators:** Experts use leading indicators (customer engagement metrics, sales pipeline quality, employee sentiment, churn cohort analysis) to detect failure before lagging indicators (revenue, profit, market share) confirm it. The guide must teach the reader to identify domain-appropriate leading indicators.
  - **The sunk cost failure:** Continuing to invest in a failing strategy because of prior investment is a well-documented judgment failure. The expert skill is knowing when additional information is worth waiting for vs. when the evidence is sufficient to conclude failure and pivot.

## PHASE 1: READER CALIBRATION

### → CALIBRATION OUTPUT:

- **Starting chapter (based on prior exposure): Chapter 1** — The reader is a complete novice in business ("I have never taken a business course, never run a P&L"). Begin at conceptual ground zero. No jargon without definition. No assumed business background. However, the reader is NOT a general novice — they are an expert-level thinker in adjacent technical domains. This means: no patronizing simplification of complex concepts, aggressive use of structural analogies from the reader's strong domains, and permission to move quickly once a concept clicks.

- **Analogy pool (domains to draw from):**
  1. **Bayesian inference and probabilistic reasoning** (PRIMARY) — Decision-making under uncertainty as Bayesian updating; business intuition as strong priors built from experience; strategy as posterior predictive checking
  2. **Linear algebra and functional analysis** (PRIMARY) — Business competencies as basis functions; different frameworks as change of basis; framework blind spots as kernel/null space; competitive advantage dimensions as eigendecomposition; different stakeholder views as orthogonal projections
  3. **Software architecture and systems engineering** (STRONG) — Organizational architecture as system architecture; debugging businesses as debugging complex systems; modularity, coupling, cohesion as org design principles; API design as the interface between departments
  4. **Computational topology** (STRONG) — Organizational invariants under growth/transformation; persistent features of competitive landscapes vs. transient noise; phase transitions at organizational scale thresholds
  5. **Visual arts and creative practice** (MODERATE) — The CEO's strategy→execution gap as the artist's vision→execution gap; aesthetic judgment as a model for tacit business judgment
  6. **AI/ML systems design** (STRONG) — Multi-objective optimization as the executive's tradeoff problem; training dynamics as organizational development; the gap between lab performance and real-world performance as the strategy→reality gap

- **Depth emphasis (based on goal): Comprehensive with depth on judgment** — The reader needs FULL landscape coverage (a business-illiterate technical founder cannot afford blind spots) with deepest treatment on executive judgment and strategic reasoning (highest-tacit-knowledge areas). Financial reasoning should be deep enough for independent business evaluation. Organizational design should be deep enough to understand why companies break at certain scales. Graduate-level engagement, not survey course.

- **What to abbreviate:** Nothing can be entirely skipped — the reader's business knowledge is at ground zero. However, concepts that map cleanly to the reader's existing mathematical/statistical knowledge (probability, uncertainty propagation, optimization under constraints) can be introduced with the analogical bridge first, then the business-specific instantiation, without belaboring the abstract structure.

- **What to expand:**
  1. **Executive judgment** — the meta-skill of synthesis under uncertainty. This is the highest-tacit, hardest-to-transmit area, and the reader's primary survival need.
  2. **Financial statement reasoning** — the reader explicitly cannot read financials. This needs full Structure A treatment from ground zero.
  3. **Organizational scaling** — the reader is building a company from near-zero and needs to understand the phase transitions ahead.
  4. **Business model evaluation** — the reader needs to assess their own startup's business model and those of competitors.
  5. **Capital allocation** — the reader is making capital allocation decisions daily without the reasoning framework.

- **Operating context:** Technical founder of an India-based, globally oriented AI startup. Guide addresses business strategy generally. Where regulatory environments, market structures, or institutional contexts materially change how reasoning applies, context dependencies will be called out (e.g., Indian venture capital vs. US VC, enterprise sales dynamics in India vs. Western markets, regulatory environments). Real executive decisions are being made daily — capital allocation, hiring, product strategy, market positioning — with no formal business training.
