# STAGE 3: RESEARCH GATE — REFERENCE BANK

## Step 3A–3F: Chapter Reference Bank

### CHAPTER 1: Financial Statements as a Language

```
SOURCE 1: Graham, B. & Dodd, D. (1934/2008). Security Analysis. 
  6th ed. McGraw-Hill.
  Annotation: The foundational text on reading financial 
  statements for investment insight. Establishes the 
  intellectual tradition of extracting strategic signal from 
  financial data, not just executing accounting procedures.
  Relevance: Foundation for the entire chapter. The "language" 
  metaphor builds on Graham & Dodd's insight that statements 
  must be READ, not just computed.

SOURCE 2: Penman, S. (2012). Financial Statement Analysis and 
  Security Valuation. 5th ed. McGraw-Hill.
  Annotation: Modern textbook treating financial statement 
  analysis as the bridge from accounting to valuation. 
  Rigorous but practically oriented.
  Relevance: Provides the analytical framework for strategic 
  reading — how to extract forward-looking signals from 
  backward-looking statements.

SOURCE 3: Fridson, M. & Alvarez, F. (2022). Financial Statement 
  Analysis: A Practitioner's Guide. 5th ed. Wiley.
  Annotation: Practitioner-oriented guide focused on how 
  financial statements can "inform, mislead, and how to get 
  behind the numbers." Emphasis on signal vs. noise.
  Relevance: Grounds the tacit knowledge component — what 
  experienced analysts see that novices miss.

SOURCE 4: Wahlen, J., Baginski, S., & Bradshaw, M. (2022). 
  Financial Reporting, Financial Statement Analysis and Valuation: 
  A Strategic Perspective. 10th ed. Cengage.
  Annotation: Explicitly integrates economics, business strategy, 
  and accounting. Treats financial reporting as communication 
  of management's strategic priorities.
  Relevance: Directly supports the chapter's strategic-signal 
  framing — statements as maps of competitive position.
```

### CHAPTER 2: Unit Economics & the Core Transaction

```
SOURCE 1: HBS Online — "What Are Unit Economics?" 
  Annotation: Harvard Business School's treatment of LTV:CAC 
  as the fundamental profitability test for startups. 
  Establishes the 3:1 benchmark.
  Relevance: Primary source for the LTV:CAC framework and 
  its role in business model evaluation.

SOURCE 2: Bessemer Venture Partners — Cloud Index / SaaS 
  Benchmarks.
  Annotation: Industry benchmarks for SaaS unit economics — 
  LTV:CAC ratios, payback periods, gross margins. Updated 
  regularly.
  Relevance: Provides concrete benchmarks that ground the 
  abstract framework in real-world numbers. [DECAY MARKER]

SOURCE 3: Ries, E. (2011). The Lean Startup. Crown Business.
  Annotation: Introduces innovation accounting — measuring 
  progress with actionable metrics (unit economics) rather 
  than vanity metrics (total revenue, total users).
  Relevance: Connects unit economics to the startup context. 
  The vanity metrics distinction directly supports H1 
  (Revenue Vanity Trap).
```

### CHAPTER 3: Constraint Structure & Resource Allocation

```
SOURCE 1: Goldratt, E. (1984). The Goal. North River Press.
  Annotation: The foundational text for Theory of Constraints. 
  Presented as a novel — the constraint cascade is developed 
  through narrative. Introduces the five focusing steps.
  Relevance: Anchors V2 (Constraint Cascade). Primary source 
  for the bottleneck-first approach to resource allocation.

SOURCE 2: Goldratt, E. (1994). It's Not Luck. North River Press.
  Annotation: Sequel to The Goal, applying TOC thinking 
  tools (evaporating clouds, current reality trees) to 
  strategic business problems beyond manufacturing.
  Relevance: Extends constraint thinking to strategy — 
  directly relevant for the reader's context.

SOURCE 3: TOC Institute — "Theory of Constraints Overview."
  Annotation: Systematic overview of TOC principles, five 
  focusing steps, and throughput accounting. 
  Relevance: Reference for the formal structure of TOC 
  when the reader wants the system beyond the narrative.
```

### CHAPTER 4: Capital Allocation & Financial Strategy

```
SOURCE 1: Thorndike, W. (2012). The Outsiders. Harvard 
  Business Review Press.
  Annotation: Profiles 8 CEOs who generated extraordinary 
  returns through disciplined capital allocation. Core thesis: 
  CEO's primary job is capital allocation, not operations.
  Relevance: Primary source for V8 (Capital Allocation 
  Identity). Case-study-rich source for tacit knowledge 
  blocks.

SOURCE 2: Buffett, W. — Berkshire Hathaway Annual Letters 
  (1965–present).
  Annotation: Decades of capital allocation reasoning from 
  the most famous practitioner. Accessible, explicit about 
  reasoning process.
  Relevance: Practitioner thinking-out-loud source. Grounds 
  the capital allocation framework in real decisions. Source 
  for tacit knowledge (podcast/interview equivalent — 
  Buffett's letters ARE his reasoning process made visible).

SOURCE 3: Mauboussin, M. (2012). The Success Equation. 
  Harvard Business Review Press.
  Annotation: Distinguishes skill from luck in business 
  outcomes. Relevant to evaluating capital allocation 
  decisions: was the return due to good allocation (skill) 
  or favorable conditions (luck)?
  Relevance: Supports the decision-quality vs. outcome-quality 
  distinction needed for honest evaluation of judgment.
```

### CHAPTER 5: Competitive Position & Moat

```
SOURCE 1: Porter, M. (1985). Competitive Advantage. Free Press.
  Annotation: The foundational text on competitive strategy. 
  Cost leadership, differentiation, focus. Five Forces 
  framework. Value chain analysis.
  Relevance: The canonical source. But the chapter must also 
  expose its null space (H2 — Framework Reification Error).

SOURCE 2: Greenwald, B. & Kahn, J. (2005). Competition 
  Demystified. Portfolio/Penguin.
  Annotation: Simplifies Porter into a practical framework 
  focused on barriers to entry as the primary source of 
  competitive advantage. More actionable than Five Forces.
  Relevance: The practical complement to Porter. Better for 
  a novice reader because it focuses on the single most 
  important question: "what prevents competitors?"

SOURCE 3: Buffett/Munger — Economic Moat concept. 
  Annotation: The moat framework as used by value investors — 
  network effects, switching costs, cost advantages, 
  intangible assets, efficient scale.
  Relevance: Connects competitive position to financial 
  valuation. Grounds the moat concept in investment reasoning.

SOURCE 4: Shapiro, C. & Varian, H. (1998). Information Rules. 
  Harvard Business School Press.
  Annotation: Foundational treatment of network effects, 
  switching costs, and information economics in strategy. 
  Pre-dates much of the platform economy but anticipates it.
  Relevance: Rigorous treatment of network effects and 
  switching costs — the reader's AI startup likely depends 
  on one or both of these mechanisms.
```

### CHAPTER 6: Business Model Design & Innovation

```
SOURCE 1: Christensen, C. (1997). The Innovator's Dilemma. 
  Harvard Business Review Press.
  Annotation: The canonical text on disruptive innovation. 
  Explains why incumbents rationally fail to respond to 
  disruption.
  Relevance: Primary source for V9 (Disruption Asymmetry). 
  Structure D chapter requires presenting BOTH sides.

SOURCE 2: King, A. & Baatartogtokh, B. (2015). "How Useful 
  Is the Theory of Disruptive Innovation?" MIT Sloan Management 
  Review, 57(1), 77–90.
  Annotation: Empirically tested Christensen's 77 cases — 
  found only 9% fully matched all 4 criteria of disruption 
  theory. Significant methodological challenge.
  Relevance: Essential counter-source for the Structure D 
  debate. Supplies the "Side B" argument.

SOURCE 3: Osterwalder, A. & Pigneur, Y. (2010). Business 
  Model Generation. Wiley.
  Annotation: The Business Model Canvas — a visual tool for 
  designing and evaluating business models. Widely adopted.
  Relevance: Practical tool for the reader to map and 
  evaluate their own startup's business model.

SOURCE 4: Lepore, J. (2014). "The Disruption Machine." 
  The New Yorker, June 23.
  Annotation: Historian's critique of disruption theory — 
  challenges empirical rigor, questions whether cases 
  actually support the theory, critiques cultural impact.
  Relevance: Provides the historical/interpretive critique 
  for the Structure D debate.
```

### CHAPTER 7: Organizational Design & Scaling

```
SOURCE 1: Dunbar, R. (2010). How Many Friends Does One Person 
  Need? Harvard University Press.
  Annotation: The research behind Dunbar's Number (150) and 
  the nested social group thresholds (5, 15, 50, 150). Based 
  on neocortex size and primate social group data.
  Relevance: Primary source for the organizational phase 
  transition thresholds in V4 (Organizational Entropy).

SOURCE 2: Conway, M. (1968). "How Do Committees Invent?" 
  Datamation, April.
  Annotation: Original statement of Conway's Law — 
  organizational structure constrains system architecture. 
  Short, foundational paper.
  Relevance: Directly relevant for the software architect 
  reader — their existing expertise (software architecture) 
  maps isomorphically to organizational architecture.

SOURCE 3: Skelton, M. & Pais, M. (2019). Team Topologies. 
  IT Revolution Press.
  Annotation: Modern treatment of Conway's Law applied to 
  team design. Stream-aligned teams, platform teams, 
  enabling teams, complicated subsystem teams.
  Relevance: Connects org design to the reader's software 
  engineering background. The Inverse Conway Maneuver.
```

### CHAPTER 8: Incentive Design & Culture

```
SOURCE 1: Kerr, S. (1975). "On the Folly of Rewarding A, 
  While Hoping for B." Academy of Management Journal, 18(4), 
  769–783.
  Annotation: Classic paper on incentive misalignment. 
  Examples from military, politics, education, medicine. 
  One of the most cited management papers ever.
  Relevance: Primary source for V7 (Incentive-Behavior 
  Isomorphism) and H8 (Metric Goodhart).

SOURCE 2: Jensen, M. & Meckling, W. (1976). "Theory of the 
  Firm: Managerial Behavior, Agency Costs and Ownership 
  Structure." Journal of Financial Economics, 3(4), 305–360.
  Annotation: Foundation of agency theory — the principal-agent 
  problem, information asymmetry, and incentive alignment 
  as the central problems of organizational design.
  Relevance: Provides the economic theory underlying incentive 
  design. Connects to V3 (Information Asymmetry Premium).

SOURCE 3: Schein, E. (2010). Organizational Culture and 
  Leadership. 4th ed. Jossey-Bass.
  Annotation: Defines organizational culture at three levels 
  (artifacts, espoused values, basic assumptions). Argues 
  culture is the most important and most difficult leadership 
  task.
  Relevance: The traditional view that the threshold concept 
  (culture-as-emergent) challenges. Essential reading for 
  understanding what the traditional view gets right and wrong.
```

### CHAPTER 9: Market & Customer Reasoning

```
SOURCE 1: Blank, S. (2005). The Four Steps to the Epiphany. 
  K&S Ranch Publishing.
  Annotation: The customer development model — get out of the 
  building, test hypotheses with real customers, iterate. 
  Foundation of the lean startup movement.
  Relevance: Directly relevant for the reader's startup. 
  Grounds V5 (Market Signal Decay) — the founder must stay 
  close to the customer signal.

SOURCE 2: Christensen, C. et al. (2016). "Know Your Customers' 
  Jobs to Be Done." Harvard Business Review, Sept.
  Annotation: The Jobs-to-Be-Done framework — customers don't 
  buy products, they hire them to do a job. Reframes market 
  reasoning around customer causality, not demographics.
  Relevance: Provides a robust framework for understanding 
  what customers actually want (vs. what they say they want).

SOURCE 3: Moore, G. (1991/2014). Crossing the Chasm. 3rd ed. 
  Harper Business.
  Annotation: The technology adoption lifecycle — early 
  adopters ≠ mainstream market. The "chasm" between them 
  is where most tech startups fail.
  Relevance: Directly relevant for the AI startup reader. 
  Addresses H5 (Scalability Presumption) — what works for 
  early adopters may not work for mainstream.
```

### CHAPTER 10: Executive Judgment & Decision Synthesis

```
SOURCE 1: Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, Fast and Slow. 
  Farrar, Straus and Giroux.
  Annotation: System 1/System 2 framework for understanding 
  cognitive biases in judgment. Anchoring, availability, 
  representativeness, overconfidence.
  Relevance: Provides the cognitive science foundation for 
  understanding WHY executive judgment fails. The reader's 
  Bayesian background makes this particularly accessible.

SOURCE 2: Tetlock, P. & Gardner, D. (2015). Superforecasting. 
  Crown.
  Annotation: How superforecasters beat experts: probabilistic 
  thinking, updating beliefs, calibration, decomposition. 
  Good Judgment Project results.
  Relevance: Primary source for V10 (Judgment Calibration 
  Principle). Connects the reader's Bayesian framework to 
  practical judgment improvement.

SOURCE 3: Tetlock, P. (2005). Expert Political Judgment. 
  Princeton University Press.
  Annotation: Famous finding that expert predictions are 
  barely better than chance. Hedgehogs (one big idea) vs. 
  foxes (many approaches). Foundational for calibration 
  research.
  Relevance: The research base for why calibration matters 
  more than confidence. Grounds the guide's emphasis on 
  epistemic humility.

SOURCE 4: Klein, G. (1998). Sources of Power: How People 
  Make Decisions. MIT Press.
  Annotation: Recognition-Primed Decision (RPD) model — 
  how experts actually make decisions in the field (not 
  through analysis but through pattern recognition + 
  mental simulation). Naturalistic decision-making research.
  Relevance: Provides the "other side" to Kahneman — expert 
  judgment is not just bias-prone, it's also powerful when 
  calibrated. Essential for the tacit knowledge treatment.
```

## Step 3G: Tacit Knowledge Source Bank

```
TACIT KNOWLEDGE SOURCE BANK

SKILL 1: Strategic Pattern Recognition

  Case study: WeWork (2019) — Failure to recognize the 
    "unit economics death spiral" pattern despite enormous 
    revenue growth. Experienced investors initially missed 
    it too (SoftBank invested $18.5B). What pattern experts 
    would have recognized: long-term fixed costs + short-term 
    variable revenue + negative unit economics = structural 
    fragility regardless of growth rate.
  Source: Multiple — WeWork S-1 filing, Reeves Brown analysis, 
    Eliot Brown & Maureen Farrell "The Cult of We" (2021).

  Simulation: "Classify the competitive situation." Present 
    the reader with 5 disguised business scenarios and ask 
    them to classify each (market entry, disruption threat, 
    consolidation, commoditization, platform competition). 
    Expert answer reveals the reasoning cues.

  Debate: Is AI fundamentally a tool (augments existing 
    businesses) or a platform (creates entirely new business 
    categories)? Credible experts disagree. Implications for 
    the reader's startup strategy differ enormously.

  Podcast/Interview: "Executive Decisions with Steve Sedgwick" 
    (CNBC) — CEOs describing pivotal choices and the reasoning 
    behind them. "All Else Equal" (Stanford GSB) — Berk and 
    van Binsbergen on decision science.

SKILL 2: Financial Signal Extraction

  Case study: Amazon annual reports (1997–2010) — Bezos' 
    letters reveal how to read financial statements for 
    strategic signal. Cash flow > earnings; customer metrics 
    > revenue metrics; infrastructure investment framed as 
    long-term competitive advantage.

  Simulation: "Read the 10-K." Give the reader a real 10-K 
    filing (sanitized/simplified if necessary) and ask them 
    to identify: (a) the binding constraint, (b) the 
    competitive moat signal, (c) the capital allocation 
    pattern, (d) the organizational health signal. Expert 
    annotated answer follows.

  Debate: Should startups optimize for GAAP profitability 
    or free cash flow? Different experts genuinely disagree, 
    and the answer depends on context (growth stage, market 
    conditions, capital availability).

  Podcast/Interview: Buffett's Berkshire Hathaway annual 
    letters — functioning as "written podcasts" where an 
    expert makes their financial reasoning process visible. 
    The Knowledge Project (Farnam Street) — episodes on 
    decision-making and financial reasoning.

SKILL 3: Organizational Diagnosis

  Case study: Nokia (2007–2013) — Organizational dysfunction 
    (fear-based culture, information suppression, middle 
    management filter) killed the company despite having 
    more smartphone engineers than Apple. The technology 
    existed; the organization couldn't deploy it.

  Simulation: "Diagnose the dysfunction." Present a set of 
    organizational symptoms (high turnover in one division, 
    slow project delivery, customer complaints about 
    responsiveness) and ask the reader to identify root 
    causes using the constraint and incentive frameworks.

  Debate: Are reorgs (organizational restructuring) ever 
    effective, or do they primarily serve as political signals 
    of "doing something" while destroying institutional 
    knowledge? Real disagreement among practitioners.

  Podcast/Interview: "The Lazy CEO Podcast" (Jim Schleckser) 
    — CEOs discussing strategic organizational decisions. 
    "Strategy and Leadership Podcast" (SME Strategy Consulting) 
    — practical org design discussions.

SKILL 4: Judgment Under Time Pressure

  Case study: Netflix's DVD-to-streaming pivot (2011) — 
    Reed Hastings' decision to split the DVD and streaming 
    businesses destroyed 75% of the stock price in the 
    short term but created a $200B+ company. The decision 
    was made under time pressure with incomplete information 
    about the streaming market's trajectory.

  Simulation: "Make the call." Present a time-pressured 
    decision scenario with insufficient information and 
    ask the reader to make a decision, state their 
    confidence level, and identify what additional 
    information would change their decision. Compare 
    actual outcome after the exercise.

  Debate: Should CEOs "decide fast and correct later" or 
    "wait for more information"? Bezos argues for 70% 
    information threshold; others argue for more patience. 
    The answer genuinely depends on reversibility.

  Podcast/Interview: "The Decision-Making Studio Podcast" — 
    long-form conversations on decision-making under 
    uncertainty. "The Thinking Leader" (Red Team Thinking) 
    — strategic thinking under pressure.

SKILL 5: Stakeholder Synthesis

  Case study: Satya Nadella's first year at Microsoft 
    (2014) — Simultaneously navigated: investor expectations 
    for growth, employee culture of "stack ranking" toxicity, 
    market position erosion to AWS/Google, and strategic 
    pivot from Windows-first to cloud-first. The synthesis 
    across all dimensions is the skill.

  Simulation: "Balance the board." Present a scenario where 
    financial metrics say "cut costs," strategic analysis 
    says "invest in R&D," organizational health says "reduce 
    workload," and market signals say "launch faster." Ask 
    the reader to make one integrated decision that addresses 
    all four signals. Expert answer reveals how synthesis 
    actually works.

  Debate: Should CEOs prioritize shareholders, employees, 
    or customers? The "stakeholder capitalism" vs. 
    "shareholder primacy" debate (Friedman vs. Freeman) 
    has real-world implications for every strategic decision.

  Podcast/Interview: "Masters of Scale" (Reid Hoffman) — 
    features iconic CEOs discussing how they balanced 
    competing priorities. "All Else Equal" (Stanford GSB) — 
    decision science applied to multi-stakeholder problems.
```
